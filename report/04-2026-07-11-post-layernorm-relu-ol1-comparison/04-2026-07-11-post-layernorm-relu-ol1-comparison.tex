\tightsection{Corrected Compute Target}

\textit{ReLU Strikes Back}~\cite{mirzadeh2023} motivates the relufication pattern. Applied to Pythia's parallel-residual block,
\begin{align*}
U_l &= \operatorname{ReLU}(\operatorname{LN}^{\mathrm{att}}_l(H_l)),
& \operatorname{Attn}_l &= \operatorname{Attention}(U_l),\\
M_l &= \operatorname{ReLU}(\operatorname{LN}^{\mathrm{mlp}}_l(H_l)),
& G_l &= \operatorname{ReLU}(M_lW_{1,l}),\\
H_{l+1} &= H_l+\operatorname{Attn}_l+G_lW_{2,l}.
\end{align*}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em]
\item \textbf{Three-ReLU} means ReLU at \code{attention\_inputs}, \code{mlp\_inputs}, and \code{mlp\_hiddens} in all six blocks. The final model LayerNorm is unchanged.
\item The three direct targets are immediately before QKV, MLP-up $W_1$, and MLP-down $W_2$.
\item \code{attention\_outputs} and \code{residual\_streams} are too late: their producer matmuls have already executed, and the next dense projection or LayerNorm does not preserve their zero mask.
\end{itemize}

Pythia-14M has $L=6$ blocks, hidden width $d=128$, MLP width $4d=512$, sequence length $T=2048$, and vocabulary size $V=50{,}304$. Let $z_a,z_m,z_h\in[0,1]$ be exact-zero fractions at the attention input, MLP input, and MLP hidden. Define
\begin{align*}
C_{\mathrm{target}}(z)&=3d^2z_a+4d^2z_m+4d^2z_h,\\
C_{\mathrm{block}}&=12d^2+d(T+1),\\
C_{\mathrm{model}}&=LC_{\mathrm{block}}+dV,\\
R_{\mathrm{block}}(z)&=\frac{C_{\mathrm{target}}(z)}{C_{\mathrm{block}}},
& R_{\mathrm{model}}(z)&=\frac{LC_{\mathrm{target}}(z)}{C_{\mathrm{model}}}.
\end{align*}
The $d(T+1)$ term is the combined valid-causal QK-score and PV-mix cost, averaged per token. $R_{\mathrm{block}}$ uses one complete transformer block as its denominator; $R_{\mathrm{model}}$ uses all six blocks plus the final $d\times V$ language-model projection. Both are potentially avoidable scalar-product fractions, not measured speedups.

For this model and sequence length:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em]
\item If all three gate inputs were zero, $C_{\mathrm{target}}(1,1,1)=11d^2=180{,}224$ products per token per block.
\item $C_{\mathrm{block}}=458{,}880$ and $C_{\mathrm{model}}=9{,}192{,}192$ products per token.
\item Therefore \textbf{$R_{\mathrm{block}}(1,1,1)=39.2748\pct{}$} and \textbf{$R_{\mathrm{model}}(1,1,1)=11.7637\pct{}$}. These are architecture ceilings; measured values use the observed $z_a,z_m,z_h$ in Table~\ref{tab:zeros}.
\end{itemize}

The ceiling falls from 39.27\pct{} block-local to 11.76\pct{} model-wide because the second denominator includes the dense, ungated final language-model projection, which dominates the matmul count in this small model.

For site $s$ and layer $l$, exact-zero percentage is
\[
z_{s,l}=100\,\frac{\sum_{b,t,j}\mathbf{1}[A^{(s,l)}_{btj}=0]}{\sum_{b,t,j}1}.
\]
The comparison is direct equality to floating-point zero on the captured forward tensor; it is not a histogram-bin estimate and does not count merely small values. Counters cover all 692,224 tokens in the deterministic validation cache, all coordinates, and all six layers. Reported site aggregates pool their integer hit and element counts.

\item \textbf{Scale effect.} $R_{\mathrm{model}}^{\max}$ rises from 11.76\pct{} at 14M to 33.97\pct{} at 70M, 54.65\pct{} at 160M, 68.55\pct{} at 410M, and 86.78\pct{} at 12B. The LM-head share simultaneously falls from 70.05\pct{} to 2.17\pct{}.

\item \textbf{Scope.} This is full-sequence, full-logit accounting at $T=2048$. It does not predict larger-model zero rates; those require training and measurement, and decoding with a KV cache has a different denominator.

\tightsection{How Exact Zeros Propagate}

The propagation path answers precisely where skip opportunities exist.

\textbf{Attention branch.} A zero coordinate in $U_l$ multiplies an entire corresponding row of each Q, K, and V weight matrix, so it creates $3d$ exactly zero input--weight products. It does \emph{not} normally create a zero Q, K, or V output coordinate: each output is a sum over the other input coordinates and a bias. QK scores are therefore dense sums; softmax is strictly positive on valid causal positions; the PV mix is dense; and the attention output projection sums dense context coordinates. Residual addition then combines a dense attention output with $H_l$ and the MLP output. The original gate zeros have saved QKV products but have not survived as output zeros.

\textbf{MLP branch.} A zero in $M_l$ creates $4d$ zero products in MLP-up. The up-projection output is again a dense sum. The following hidden ReLU creates a \emph{new} exact-zero mask $G_l$, whose zeros create $d$ zero products each in MLP-down. The down-projection output is dense, and residual addition is dense. Thus MLP-input and MLP-hidden zeros are two distinct skip interfaces, not one mask propagating through both matrices.

The heatmaps therefore reject a ``zeros cascade through the layer'' interpretation. Sparsity is local and consumable only at the matmul immediately following each gate; the hidden MLP ReLU creates a second independent mask.

\tightsection{Activation, Weight, and LayerNorm Distributions}

Exact-zero rates do not describe the nonzero tail. Three-site pressure also changes activation scale and can create a reservoir of small positive values. For example, relative to Three-ReLU AdamW, OL1 reduces pooled activation RMS from 0.723 to 0.172 at attention inputs, 0.705 to 0.148 at MLP inputs, and 0.115 to 0.020 at MLP hiddens. This distinction matters: a small positive activation is not free in an exact sparse kernel, and clipping can show that some near-zero mass is functionally important.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../figures/88-pythia-14m-minipile-post-layernorm-relu-activation-weight-densities.pdf}
\caption{Activation and immediately downstream weight densities for the control ladder, using shared method colors. Pairing each activation with the matrix that consumes it distinguishes hard gates, small-positive reservoirs, and weight adaptation. Exact-zero point masses are reported separately from direct counters rather than inferred from continuous density estimates.}
\end{figure}

The downstream weights adapt but do not become sparse. In the original matched comparison, OL1 versus Three-ReLU AdamW increases QKV weight RMS from 0.0433 to 0.0493, MLP-up from 0.0219 to 0.0235, and MLP-down from 0.0218 to 0.0251. This broadening is associated with smaller activation scales, but the observational density plot cannot establish that weights causally compensate for sparse activations.

The branch LayerNorm is
\[
\operatorname{LN}(x)=\gamma\odot\frac{x-\mu(x)}{\sqrt{\operatorname{var}(x)+\epsilon}}+\beta,
\qquad A=\operatorname{ReLU}(\operatorname{LN}(x)).
\]
Here $\gamma$ controls normalized scale and $\beta$ moves coordinates across the zero gate. OL1 changes both: relative to config 98, mean attention-LayerNorm $\gamma$ moves from 1.017 to 0.870 and mean $\beta$ from +0.017 to $-0.135$; mean MLP-LayerNorm $\gamma$ moves from 1.003 to 0.918 and mean $\beta$ from +0.003 to $-0.089$. These parameter shifts explain how a fixed zero threshold can acquire very different site- and layer-specific gate rates.

\noindent\textbf{TODO --- Learnable zero-threshold activation.}
The proposed follow-up makes the ReLU zero boundary an explicit model parameter instead of asking LayerNorm to move activations around a fixed boundary. Let $z_l^{(s)}$ enter gate $s\in\{\text{attention input},\text{MLP input},\text{MLP hidden}\}$ in layer $l$. It is the corresponding LayerNorm output at the first two sites and the MLP-up output at the hidden site. Define
\[
g_{\tau_l^{(s)}}(z)=
\begin{cases}
0, & z\leq\tau_l^{(s)},\\
z, & z>\tau_l^{(s)},
\end{cases}
\qquad
A_l^{(s)}=g_{\tau_l^{(s)}}\!\left(z_l^{(s)}\right).
\]
This hard gate leaves surviving values unchanged. At $\tau_l^{(s)}=0$ it is exactly the current ReLU; $\tau_l^{(s)}>0$ creates more exact zeros by removing small positive values. A negative threshold would pass negative values, so the first test should constrain $\tau_l^{(s)}\geq0$. The symbol $\tau$ avoids collision with LayerNorm scale $\gamma$.

The TODO has three concrete parts:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em]
  \item \textbf{Parameterization.} Initialize one scalar $\tau_l^{(s)}=0$ per layer/site and learn it jointly with the model, allowing each gate to choose a different zero rate without per-coordinate thresholds.
  \item \textbf{Optimization.} Use a global, matmul-cost-weighted exact-zero budget. Because the hard boundary has no useful derivative with respect to $\tau$ away from the boundary, compare smooth or straight-through training with alternating hard-threshold calibration.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation.} Compare fixed Three-ReLU, post-hoc clipping, and learned thresholds at matched validation loss and $R_{\mathrm{model}}$. Report learned thresholds, exact-zero rates by layer/site, the clipping frontier, logical product reduction, and measured sparse-kernel latency.
\end{itemize}
Figure~\ref{fig:layernorm-parameters} motivates this TODO: $\gamma$ and $\beta$ shift after post-LayerNorm ReLU, showing that the model already changes the distribution around the fixed zero boundary. This association does not establish that a learned threshold will help; the proposed experiment asks whether controlling the boundary directly yields a better quality--compute frontier. No learnable-threshold run has been performed in this study.

\tightsection{Post-hoc Clipping: Robustness and the Compute Frontier}

Clipping sweeps use the complete validation cache and replace values with zero when $|a|\leq t$. Threshold zero is the unmodified checkpoint. Site-specific sweeps isolate which near-zero reservoirs are safe; joint sweeps clip all three gates and convert resulting exact zeros to the model-wide denominator above.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../figures/82-pythia-14m-minipile-post-layernorm-relu-site-clipping-frontiers.pdf}
\caption{Site-specific clipping frontiers. Validation-loss change is measured from each checkpoint's own threshold-zero loss, separating clipping fragility from training quality. The panels show that equal near-zero mass does not imply equal removable computation across attention input, MLP input, and MLP hidden.}
\end{figure}

The earlier OL1 result illustrates the point sharply: at $t=0.01$, attention-input clipping adds only 0.00034 validation loss and MLP-input clipping adds 0.00719, whereas MLP-hidden clipping adds 0.8074. Its large MLP-hidden $|a|\leq0.01$ reservoir is therefore not a safe exact-zero operating point.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../figures/83-pythia-14m-minipile-post-layernorm-relu-joint-compute-frontier.pdf}
\caption{Joint three-site clipping frontier against potentially avoidable \emph{model-wide} matmul products. The denominator is six complete transformer blocks plus the unprunable final language-model projection at $T=2048$; therefore $R_{\mathrm{model}}(1,1,1)=11.7637\pct{}$ is the architecture ceiling. These are logical products, not dense-PyTorch speedups.}
\end{figure}

OR owns the useful pressured frontier in this test. Its unmodified checkpoint starts at 8.5856\pct{} potentially avoidable model matmuls and loss 5.0091. Clipping at $t=0.003$ reaches 8.6208\pct{} with a numerically 0.00009 lower loss; $t=0.01$ reaches 8.6882\pct{} for only +0.00179 loss. L1N starts at 6.8006\pct{} and needs $t=0.01$ to reach 8.3551\pct{}, at a +0.5823 loss cost. The frontier therefore confirms the direct counters: balanced sparsity at all three compute-facing sites matters more than maximizing a single site's zero rate.

The head-inclusive percentage is deliberately smaller than the 28.66\pct{} OR block number. The final vocabulary projection dominates this small model's full-logit matmul count and has no ReLU gate. Both denominators are useful: $R_{\mathrm{block}}$ isolates the mechanism inside a transformer block, while $R_{\mathrm{model}}$ prevents that local opportunity from being presented as whole-model savings.

\tightsection{Conclusions and Recommended Next Step}

The current evidence supports six conclusions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em]
  \item \textbf{Architecture.} Post-LayerNorm ReLUs create genuine, directly consumable zeros before QKV and MLP-up, but the tested Three-ReLU architecture costs 0.1160 validation loss relative to MLP-ReLU AdamW.
  \item \textbf{Pressure result is provisional.} OR produces the best balanced three-gate result among the completed runs. However, the matched Three-ReLU RN control---naive Ricker without Adam-step orthogonalization---has not been run because of GPU/time constraints. The present experiment therefore cannot separate the effect of Ricker pressure itself from the effect of orthogonalization.
  \item \textbf{Propagation is local.} Each existing ReLU gate exposes products only in its immediately downstream QKV, MLP-up, or MLP-down matmul. Dense projections and residual addition do not preserve the scalar zero mask; the MLP-hidden ReLU creates a new mask.
  \item \textbf{QK and PV are open targets.} In the current block, Q, K, and V are dense after the QKV projection. RoPE then rotates pairs of Q/K coordinates: it does not need dense values merely to know token position, but an isolated scalar zero is generally mixed with its paired coordinate, while an all-zero pair remains zero. Standard softmax probabilities are positive at valid causal positions, so plain ReLU on $P$ would not sparsify $PV$.
  \item \textbf{TODO: make the zero threshold learnable.} The LayerNorm $\gamma$/$\beta$ shifts in Figure~\ref{fig:layernorm-parameters} show that the distribution moves around a fixed ReLU boundary. A nonnegative $\tau_l^{(s)}$, initialized at zero and learned per layer/site under a global compute budget, would make this sparsity decision explicit. This is a proposed experiment, not a result.
  \item \textbf{Scale changes the ceiling.} The low absolute $R_{\mathrm{model}}$ at 14M is strongly architecture-limited: OR already uses 73.0\pct{} of its ceiling, and the ceiling rises sharply with Pythia scale.
\end{itemize}

The research agenda should now move from local sparsity maximization to a global, hardware-aware activation budget:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Complete the missing RN control.} Add and run a Three-ReLU RN full-pass configuration matched to OR in initialization, data order, token budget, and Ricker parameters $w=1$, $c=\sigma=0.05$. This is the required comparison for attributing any gain to Ricker pressure versus Adam-step orthogonalization. Keep OR and MLP-only OL1 (config 81) as references; do not prioritize L1N or Three-ReLU OL1 at their present weights. This run was deferred only by GPU/time constraints.
  \item \textbf{Separate the architecture cost.} Train attention-input-only and MLP-input-only post-LayerNorm ReLU ablations against MLP-ReLU AdamW. Keep the final LayerNorm untouched.
  \item \textbf{TODO: learn where to spend zeros.} Replace equal site averaging with model-matmul cost weights and nonnegative $g_{\tau}$ gates initialized as ordinary ReLUs. Start with one threshold per layer/site and one global compute budget; compare smooth or straight-through training with alternating hard-threshold calibration, fixed ReLU, and post-hoc clipping.
